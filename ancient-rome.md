# The Rise and Fall of Ancient Rome

Rome wasn't built in a day, and it definitely didn't fall in one either. The Roman Empire is one of the most studied civilizations in history, and for good reason — it shaped so much of the world we live in today.

## The Republic

Rome started as a small city-state in Italy around 753 BC (according to legend, it was founded by Romulus and Remus). For centuries, it was a republic where elected officials called consuls held power. The Senate was the main governing body, and Roman citizens had a say in how things were run.

The republic expanded aggressively, eventually controlling most of the Mediterranean. But internal conflicts, civil wars, and power struggles between generals like Julius Caesar and Pompey eventually brought the republic to an end.

## The Empire

After Caesar's assassination in 44 BC, his adopted son Octavian (later called Augustus) became the first Roman Emperor in 27 BC. This marked the beginning of the Roman Empire, which would last for nearly 500 years in the west.

At its peak, the empire stretched from Britain in the north to Egypt in the south, and from Spain in the west to Mesopotamia in the east. The Romans built roads, aqueducts, and buildings that still stand today. Latin, the Roman language, evolved into the Romance languages (French, Spanish, Italian, Portuguese, Romanian).

## The Fall

The Western Roman Empire fell in 476 AD when the last emperor, Romulus Augustulus, was deposed by a Germanic chieftain named Odoacer. The reasons for the fall are still debated by historians — some point to barbarian invasions, others to economic problems, political corruption, or overexpansion.

The Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantine Empire) survived for almost another thousand years, finally falling to the Ottoman Turks in 1453.

## Why It Matters

Roman law, engineering, language, and political ideas influenced basically every Western civilization that came after. Concepts like the republic, the senate, and citizens' rights all trace back to Rome. Even modern road systems follow paths originally laid out by Roman engineers.
